What is Medicaid?

Medicaid is a public health insurance program in the United States that provides coverage for low-income individuals and families. It helps pay for medical services like doctor visits, hospital stays, long-term care, and more. The program is jointly funded by the federal government and individual state governments.

How Do You Qualify?

Eligibility for Medicaid depends on several factors, including income level, household size, disability status, and age. While requirements vary by state, the program generally covers:

  • Low-income adults
  • Children and pregnant women
  • Seniors and people with disabilities

We Must Continue Funding Medicaid

Medicaid is a lifeline for millions of Americans. Preserving and strengthening it is essential to ensuring a fair and functional healthcare system.

Medicaid Funding & Payments — U.S. Overview
How It's Funded

Medicaid is jointly funded by:

  • Federal Government (~60% average)
  • State Governments (~40%)

Funding ratio depends on the Federal Medical Assistance Percentage (FMAP), ranging from 50% to 83%, based on state income levels.

Where the Money Goes
  • Low-income adults and families
  • Children’s healthcare
  • Pregnant women
  • People with disabilities
  • Elderly in nursing homes
Top Spending Areas
  • Long-term services and supports (LTSS)
  • Acute care (doctor visits, hospital stays)
  • Prescription drugs
