Social Security Wage Base

The income cap for Social Security taxes is called the "Social Security Wage Base", also known as the "Contribution and Benefit Base."

What Does It Mean?

  • The Social Security Wage Base is the maximum income subject to Social Security payroll taxes.
  • Wages and self-employment income earned above this cap are not subject to the 6.2% Social Security tax (12.4% for self-employed).

2024 Example:

  • The Social Security Wage Base for 2024 is $168,600.
  • If you earn $200,000, you only pay Social Security taxes on the first $168,600.
  • The 6.2% tax on $168,600 results in a maximum contribution of $10,453.20 from employees, with employers matching this amount.
  • Medicare taxes have no wage base limit, meaning the 1.45% Medicare tax (2.9% for self-employed) applies to all income, with an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on earnings over $200,000 ($250,000 for married couples filing jointly).

Why Is This Important?

  • The cap limits how much high earners contribute to Social Security, leading to debates about fairness and system funding.
  • Proposals to raise or eliminate the cap aim to increase revenue for Social Security, potentially extending its solvency.

Potential Impact of Raising the Wage Base to $1 Million

Raising the Social Security Wage Base to $1 million could generate approximately $13.38 billion in additional revenue annually. This change would enhance the solvency of the Social Security program and provide a more equitable distribution of payroll taxes among high earners. It is estimated that this change could extend the solvency of the Social Security Trust Fund to approximately 2075.
